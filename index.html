Inferring Intention and Team Goal to Enable Proactive Robot Actions in Non-dyadic Human-Robot Teams

Rabeya Jamshad*, Mainak Pal*, Vijay Gupta, Laurel D. Riek
*equal contribution

In teams, effective coordination requires people to coordinate implicitly. During implicit coordination, team members anticipate each other’s intentions, and adapt their own actions to achieve shared goals. As robots are introduced in teams, they must also infer the team goal and intentions through observed actions. Our work proposes an intention and goal inference method based on Bayesian Theory of Mind. This work will enable robots to support fluent human-robot coordination in non-dyadic teams.

Designing Proactive Robots for Non-dyadic Teams in Dynamic Contexts

Rabeya Jamshad, Arthi Haripriyan, Advika Sonti, Preeti Ramaraj, Susan Simkins (Penn State), Laurel D. Riek

Teams are essential to how we accomplish challenging tasks in dynamic environments such as healthcare, hospitality and emergency response. As robots are introduced in these environments, they must learn to fluently collaborate with their teammates and positively influence team dynamics. In our work, we design robots that anticipate team and task needs to proactively assist their teammates. We also consider how the robot’s actions influence intra-team dynamics and how we can adapt robot behavior to the task context.

Design of Telemedical Robots to Mitigate Workflow Disruptions in High Workload Environments

Sachiko Matsumoto, Rabeya Jamshad, Pratyusha Ghosh, Laurel D. Riek

In emergency medicine departments, healthcare workers often multitask and face frequent interruptions while providing patient care. This can directly impact patient outcomes through increased risk of errors and preventable patient harm. In our work, we are collaborating with healthcare workers to design robots that can support healthcare workers with interruption-mitigation and managing their workflows.

Design of a Crib Mobile to Support Studies in the Early Detection of Cerebral Palsy

Rabeya Jamshad, Katelyn E Fry, Yu-ping Chen, Ayanna Howard

Early detection of neuromuscular disorders such as cerebral palsy is essential to provide timely intervention to at-risk infants. In our work, we design and develop robots that can introduce stimuli to encourage kicking movements in preverbal infants. These stimuli are introduced based on a goal and reward mechanism. We conduct at-home studies to validate the effectiveness of our stimuli and the robotic intervention.
